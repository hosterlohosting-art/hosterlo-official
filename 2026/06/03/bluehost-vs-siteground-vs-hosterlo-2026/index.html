Bluehost — Good Marketing, Mixed Reality

Bluehost is arguably the most recognized hosting brand in the English-speaking world — and that recognition is almost entirely the product of one of the most aggressive affiliate marketing programs in the industry. WordPress.org officially "recommended" Bluehost for years, a recommendation that was widely questioned as influenced by financial arrangements. In 2026, that official recommendation has been removed, and many of the users who signed up on the back of it are now questioning whether they got what they paid for.

The core issue with Bluehost is the intro-to-renewal pricing gap. That $2.95/month plan looks phenomenal on a landing page. Then your two-year term ends and the renewal invoice arrives: $10.99 to $13.99 per month for the same plan. For small business owners who signed up without reading the small print, this is a jarring shock — and many simply pay it because migrating feels complicated.

Beyond pricing, Bluehost operates on shared infrastructure under Newfold Digital (formerly EIG), a conglomerate that owns dozens of hosting brands. This shared resource model — where thousands of sites compete for the same server resources — leads to inconsistent performance, particularly during peak traffic periods. Users on basic plans frequently report sluggish TTFB (time to first byte) numbers that would concern any SEO-conscious webmaster.

That said, Bluehost genuinely excels in one area: beginner onboarding. The cPanel interface is familiar, the WordPress one-click install works seamlessly, and their customer support via phone (a rarity in modern hosting) can be reassuring for non-technical users. If you've never launched a website before and you just need something that works today with minimum friction, Bluehost will get you there.

SiteGround — Premium Speed, Premium Price

SiteGround has a well-deserved reputation among developers and performance-focused users. After migrating to Google Cloud infrastructure in 2020, the company left behind traditional dedicated servers in favor of distributed, scalable cloud architecture — and the speed improvements were real and measurable. SiteGround sites consistently post sub-200ms TTFB numbers from their primary data center regions, which is genuinely impressive for a shared hosting environment.

Their proprietary caching system — SuperCacher — combined with SG Optimizer (their WordPress plugin) delivers excellent page load times out of the box. The staging environment, available on higher-tier plans, is a feature that developers genuinely love: you can test theme or plugin changes in isolation before pushing them live. Managed WordPress auto-updates add another layer of convenience and security for site owners who don't want to babysit their CMS.

Where SiteGround runs into trouble is pricing and storage. The entry-level StartUp plan at $2.99/month renews at a staggering $17.99/month — making it one of the most aggressively repriced hosting products on the market. When you factor in year-over-year, SiteGround is easily the most expensive option on this list. Their storage limits are also unusually tight: 10 GB on the basic plan, 20 GB on GrowBig. For content-heavy sites, e-commerce stores, or anyone running multiple sites, this becomes a real constraint.

Another point: SiteGround does not include a free domain. You'll need to register or transfer your domain separately and pay for it, typically $15–$20 per year. This strips away some of the perceived value compared to Bluehost and Hosterlo. The GrowBig plan (required to host multiple sites and access staging) regularly runs $5.99–$7.99/month intro, renewing at $27.99/month — a cost that many users describe as "impossible to justify" against alternatives.

NVMe SSD vs SATA SSD is the most underappreciated performance variable in shared hosting. NVMe drives deliver sequential read speeds of 3,500+ MB/s versus the 550 MB/s ceiling of SATA SSDs. When your server is handling dozens of simultaneous database queries for WordPress — fetching post content, sidebar widgets, comment counts — that storage throughput difference translates directly to faster response times.

LiteSpeed vs Apache is the other major differentiator. LiteSpeed is an event-driven web server that handles concurrent connections with dramatically lower memory overhead than Apache's process-per-connection model. For shared hosting environments where server resources are shared, LiteSpeed maintains consistent performance under load where Apache-based servers begin to queue and slow. Hosterlo's LiteSpeed Cache plugin also eliminates redundant PHP execution for WordPress pages, serving cached HTML directly from memory for returning visitors.

SiteGround's Google Cloud infrastructure gives it a genuine edge over Bluehost's traditional data center model, but Hosterlo's combination of NVMe storage, LiteSpeed server, and globally distributed CDN edges out all competitors in our benchmark testing. The practical difference: a WordPress site on Hosterlo typically achieves a Google PageSpeed Insights mobile score 15–25 points higher than the equivalent site on Bluehost — with no additional optimization work required.

WordPress Performance Deep Dive

WordPress powers nearly 43% of all websites on the internet — so how a host treats WordPress matters enormously. Hosterlo's WordPress hosting is built on LiteSpeed Web Server with LSCache (LiteSpeed Cache) pre-configured.
